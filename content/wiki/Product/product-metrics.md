# Product Metrics

## Overview
Product metrics are quantitative measures used to evaluate how well a product is performing for its users and business. They translate goals like growth, engagement, and retention into numbers that teams can track over time. Choosing the right metrics keeps a team focused on outcomes rather than output.

## Core Concepts
- **Acquisition**: How many new users discover and start using the product.
- **Activation**: Whether users reach the product's core value quickly after signing up.
- **Retention**: The rate at which users return over time, indicating lasting value.
- **Referral**: How often existing users bring in new ones.
- **Revenue**: The monetary outcomes tied to product usage.

## How It Works
Teams begin by defining the product's goals and the questions they need to answer. They then select a small set of key performance indicators—often following frameworks like AARRR (pirate metrics)—and instrument the product to collect the underlying events. Dashboards visualize trends, and experiments like A/B tests compare variations against a baseline. Metrics are reviewed regularly to catch regressions and spot opportunities, with guardrail metrics ensuring improvements in one area don't harm another.

## Advantages
- Grounds decisions in evidence rather than opinion.
- Enables fast feedback through experimentation.
- Creates a shared language for progress across teams.
- Surfaces problems early through trend monitoring.

## Limitations
- Vanity metrics can flatter without informing decisions.
- Correlation is often mistaken for causation.
- Instrumentation and data quality require sustained investment.
- Over-optimizing for a single metric can harm the overall experience.

## Example
A team building a note-taking app tracks activation as "created first note within 5 minutes of sign-up." When onboarding changes cause this metric to drop, they investigate the funnel and discover a confusing step, then iterate to fix it.
