# User Journey

## Overview
A user journey maps the sequence of steps a person takes to accomplish a specific goal with a product, from initial awareness through to completion and beyond. It captures actions, thoughts, emotions, and pain points at each stage, revealing where the experience succeeds or breaks down. Journey maps are grounded in real research rather than assumptions about how users behave.

## Core Concepts
- **Persona**: A representative user whose journey is being mapped, based on research data.
- **Stages**: High-level phases of the experience, such as discovery, onboarding, use, and retention.
- **Touchpoints**: Specific interactions between the user and the product or brand.
- **Emotional Curve**: A visualization of how the user feels at each step.
- **Opportunities**: Gaps and friction points where the experience can be improved.

## How It Works
Mapping starts by selecting a persona and a clear goal they want to achieve. The team then breaks the experience into stages and lists the actions, thoughts, and emotions at each touchpoint. Research—interviews, analytics, and observation—fills in the map with evidence rather than guesses. The completed map highlights pain points and moments of delight, which translate into prioritized opportunities for design and engineering. Maps are revisited as the product changes and new data arrives.

## Advantages
- Builds empathy by making the user's perspective tangible.
- Aligns cross-functional teams around a shared view of the experience.
- Surfaces hidden friction that metrics alone miss.
- Prioritizes improvements by linking them to real user pain.

## Limitations
- Only as accurate as the research behind it; assumption-based maps mislead.
- Represents an average path and may obscure edge cases.
- Can become stale quickly if not maintained.
- Time-intensive to produce and validate properly.

## Example
A new user discovering a note-taking app might first see an ad, visit the landing page, sign up, create their first note, and invite a collaborator. Mapping this journey reveals that the gap between sign-up and first note creation is where most users drop off, guiding the team to improve onboarding.
