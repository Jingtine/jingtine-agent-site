# Design Thinking

## Overview
Design thinking is a human-centered methodology for solving complex problems through empathy, experimentation, and iteration. It prioritizes understanding users deeply before proposing solutions, and treats prototypes as tools for learning rather than finished products. The approach is widely used in product design, service design, and organizational change.

## Core Concepts
- **Empathy**: Understanding users' needs, behaviors, and emotions through research.
- **Problem Definition**: Framing the right question before seeking answers.
- **Ideation**: Generating a wide range of possible solutions without premature judgment.
- **Prototyping**: Building tangible, low-fidelity representations to explore ideas.
- **Testing**: Putting prototypes in front of users and learning from their reactions.

## How It Works
The process moves through five non-linear stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test. Teams start by immersing themselves in users' contexts to build empathy, then synthesize findings into a clear problem statement. They generate many ideas, build rough prototypes of the most promising ones, and test them with real users. Insights from testing loop back into earlier stages, so the process is iterative rather than strictly sequential. Each cycle refines both the problem understanding and the proposed solution.

## Advantages
- Reduces risk by validating ideas with users before heavy investment.
- Encourages creative, divergent thinking before converging on a solution.
- Builds shared understanding across diverse team members.
- Adapts to problems that are ill-defined or evolving.

## Limitations
- Can feel slow and unstructured to teams used to linear planning.
- Quality depends heavily on access to real users for research.
- Risk of superficial application—"design theater"—without genuine iteration.
- Outcomes are hard to predict, which can unsettle stakeholders.

## Example
A team designing a study-planning tool interviews students about how they manage deadlines, discovers that existing tools ignore emotional stress, and prototypes a feature that surfaces gentle reminders. Testing reveals which reminder tone actually helps, guiding the final design.
