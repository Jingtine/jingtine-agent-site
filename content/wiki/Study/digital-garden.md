# Digital Garden

## Overview
A digital garden is a personal knowledge base where notes grow and interconnect over time, in contrast to chronological blogs. It treats knowledge as a living ecosystem rather than a stream of finished posts. Gardeners tend their notes incrementally, refining and linking them as understanding deepens.

## Core Concepts
- **Interconnection** — notes link to one another with `[[wikilinks]]`, forming a graph of related ideas.
- **Stages of growth** — notes progress from seedling (rough) to budding (refining) to evergreen (mature).
- **Non-linear** — there is no timeline; visitors enter through any note and follow links organically.
- **Ownership** — the gardener curates, revises, and weaves notes into a coherent personal context.
- **Public by default** — many gardens are openly readable, inviting serendipitous discovery.

## How It Works
A gardener captures ideas as individual notes, each focused on a single concept. Notes are tagged and cross-linked using bidirectional links so that relationships between ideas become explicit. Over time the gardener revisits notes, expanding, splitting, and connecting them as their understanding evolves. The result is a graph of knowledge that mirrors how the gardener actually thinks. Tools like Obsidian, Roam, or static-site generators with wikilink support are commonly used.

## Advantages
- Encourages deep, long-term thinking rather than disposable publishing.
- Surfacing connections reveals insights that isolated notes would hide.
- Notes compound in value as the graph grows denser.
- Low pressure: notes need not be polished to be useful.
- Public gardens share nuanced, evolving understanding with others.

## Limitations
- Requires sustained maintenance or gardens become stale and overgrown.
- Discoverability is harder than on a chronological blog.
- Tooling for bidirectional links and graph views can be complex.
- Visitors may feel lost without curated entry points or indexes.
- Quality varies widely across notes of different maturity.

## Example
A student studying operating systems creates a seedling note on virtual memory, later links it to notes on paging and segmentation, and eventually weaves it into an evergreen note explaining memory hierarchy. A reader arriving at the virtual memory note can follow links outward to related concepts, building a richer mental model than a single article would allow.
