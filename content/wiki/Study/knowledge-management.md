# Knowledge Management

## Overview
Knowledge management (KM) is the discipline of creating, organizing, and applying knowledge so that the right information is available at the right time. In a personal context—often called personal knowledge management (PKM)—it covers the tools and habits an individual uses to capture ideas and retrieve them later. Effective KM turns scattered information into reusable understanding.

## Core Concepts
- **Capture** — gathering ideas, quotes, and references before they are forgotten.
- **Organize** — structuring notes with tags, folders, or links so they remain findable.
- **Distill** — refining raw notes into summaries and actionable insights.
- **Retrieve** — locating relevant knowledge quickly when needed.
- **Share** — making knowledge available to collaborators or the public.

## How It Works
A typical KM workflow begins with frictionless capture: quick notes are recorded wherever you are. Captured items are then processed into a permanent store, tagged and linked to related notes. Periodically the store is reviewed and distilled, pruning duplicates and sharpening key ideas. When a problem arises, retrieval—via search, tags, or following links—surfaces the relevant context. The cycle repeats, keeping the knowledge base current and trustworthy.

## Advantages
- Reduces information loss and repeated research.
- Builds a compounding personal library over time.
- Supports better decisions by surfacing prior context.
- Scales an individual's effective memory and expertise.
- Encourages reflective, structured thinking.

## Limitations
- Upfront effort to set up tools and habits can be high.
- Poor organization leads to a "graveyard" of unused notes.
- Tool lock-in can make migration painful.
- Maintenance discipline is required to keep content fresh.
- Over-capture without distillation creates noise, not knowledge.

## Example
A software engineer keeps a daily capture inbox for snippets, bug solutions, and reading notes. Each weekend they process the inbox, linking new notes to project pages and tagging them by topic. When a similar bug recurs months later, a quick search surfaces the prior solution and its linked context, saving hours of re-investigation.
