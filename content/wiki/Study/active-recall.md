# Active Recall

## Overview
Active recall is a learning method where you deliberately retrieve information from memory rather than passively re-reading it. It is one of the most evidence-backed study techniques in cognitive psychology. The act of pulling information out—rather than cramming it in—signals the brain that the material is worth retaining.

## Core Concepts
- **Retrieval practice** — recalling facts or concepts from memory without looking.
- **Testing effect** — the finding that retrieval itself strengthens memory more than review.
- **Spaced repetition** — scheduling recall at increasing intervals to combat forgetting.
- **Feedback** — checking answers to correct errors and reinforce correct recall.
- **Effortful learning** — desirable difficulty that makes retrieval more effective.

## How It Works
After studying material, the learner closes the source and attempts to recall key points from memory, perhaps by writing a summary or answering flashcards. Incorrect or incomplete recalls are noted and corrected against the source. Each successful retrieval reinforces the memory trace and slows future decay. When combined with spaced repetition systems (SRS), recalls are scheduled just before the learner is likely to forget, maximizing efficiency. Over sessions, material moves from fragile short-term memory into durable long-term memory.

## Advantages
- Strongly supported by decades of memory research.
- Far more efficient than passive re-reading or highlighting.
- Exposes gaps in understanding that review conceals.
- Builds transferable retrieval cues, aiding application of knowledge.
- Pairs naturally with spaced repetition for compounding gains.

## Limitations
- More cognitively demanding and less comfortable than passive review.
- Requires honest self-checking or good question banks to be effective.
- Poorly written questions can test recognition rather than true recall.
- Initial setup of flashcards or quizzes takes time.
- Overuse on trivia can crowd out deeper conceptual understanding.

## Example
A student learning networking creates flashcards: one side asks "What are the layers of the OSI model?", the other lists them. Each day the card is reviewed from memory; correct recalls push the next review further out. Within weeks the seven layers are recalled instantly and durably, far outperforming repeated re-reading of the textbook chapter.
